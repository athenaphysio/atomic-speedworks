At a glance

Six small things to try across a season

Whatever the sport — your key reminders for the drive home.

1

"I love watching you train" once, on a journey home. Resist the urge to repeat it every week — it loses its weight.

2

Pick three of the eleven questions (below) and stick with the same three for a whole season. Children find a constant rotation reassuring, not boring.

3

Don't coach at home. Spot something technical? Mention it to their coach, not them — and trust the coach to pick it up.

4

Tell your child what you noticed about their effort, attitude or energy, not their performance. Specific, sincere noticing beats generic praise every time.

5

When a progress report or end-of-block review arrives, ask what they took from it before discussing the numbers. Curiosity first, evaluation second.

6

Notice the urge to compare to a teammate, a sibling, or last season. Then pick a different question instead.

"I love watching you train."

These six habits cost nothing and take less than a minute each. Together they are, by far, the highest-leverage thing a parent can do across a season.

What the research suggests

Behaviour-change research suggests a handful of small, specific, repeatable actions beat one big, vague intention. You don't need a relationship overhaul — you need a few default responses you can reach for in the seconds after a tough session or a great one.

  • Default to curiosity before judgement.
  • Praise the process, not the outcome.
  • Let silence do some of the work — not every car journey needs analysis.
Why this guide exists

Training is half the process

The questions you ask after a training session shape how much your child actually takes from it. The pitch and the gym do half the work. The conversations you have at home do the other half.

This guide pulls together what years of treating young athletes, and the work of the best people writing about parent communication in sport, tells us about that other half. None of it is complicated. Most of it is about saying less, and choosing what you say with care.

What good coaching looks like

A quick orientation on the principles behind well-run youth training, in any sport, so the next conversation with their coach makes more sense.

1

Skill is taught, not endured.

More reps done tired, with poor technique, doesn't make a player better. Good coaches start with fun and play, then add intent and speed.

2

Form before intensity.

Early sessions in any new skill should be about the movement itself. Times, scores and reps come later, once the pattern is sound.

3

Recovery is part of the work.

Quality reps need full recovery between them. If a young athlete is out of breath doing technical work, the rest was too short — the body was training endurance, not the skill.

4

Process over outcome.

Good coaching cues describe the action — "push the ground away," "stick the landing," "tall posture" — not the result. Praise for what the player did tops praise for what they achieved.

5

Confidence comes from competence.

Generic encouragement ("great job!") fades fast. Confidence that lasts is built from genuinely getting better at something specific and noticing it for yourself. That is why good coaches break skills into small, achievable steps.

6

Play stays protected.

However serious the programme, the best coaches keep an element of enjoyment and choice in every session. Athletes who experience sport as relentlessly serious burn out younger than those who don't.

None of this needs you to become an expert. It just helps to know roughly what good practice looks like so you can ask your child's coach an informed question if something doesn't feel right.

Supporter, not coach

You are not your child's coach. Someone else is. This sounds obvious — it is, in practice, one of the hardest principles for sport parents to hold to.

The temptation to coach — from the touchline, from the car, at the breakfast table — comes from love. You want them to do well. You see something that could be tweaked, and say it. Almost always with kindness.

The problem is that for a young athlete, the parent voice and the coach voice get tangled. They start hearing technical correction at home, performance evaluation in the car, and emotional support from people they trust less than you. The relationships get muddled, and the home stops feeling like a safe place to land after a hard session.

Richard Shorter, the sports parent educator, has spent years on this question. His clearest finding: the drive home from training is often the single most important, and most dangerous, conversation in youth sport. Children replay it for the rest of the evening. Parents tend to use it for analysis, opinion or correction right when the child is tired and least able to absorb it. The technical work, the goal-setting and the strategy belong with the coach — the car is for everything else.

The best thing you can do as a sport parent is to choose what you DON'T say.

I LOVE WATCHING YOU TRAIN.

Research from Bruce Brown and Rob Miller asked thousands of young athletes one question: what do you most want to hear from your parents after a game or session? The answer was almost universal — "I love to watch you play." No performance comment or technical note, no comparison. If your child's sport is mostly training rather than match-play, the version that works just as well is "I love watching you train." Said once. It tells your child the relationship isn't contingent on results.

What this looks like in practice

  • On the way home: ask one open question, then let the radio do some of the talking.
  • After a tough loss or a flat session: resist the post-mortem until the next day, if at all.
  • At the dinner table: ask about a teammate's funny moment before you ask about the scoreboard.
  • Spot a technical fix that's needed? Mention it to the coach, not your child.
Conversation starters

Questions that build a curious athlete

Eleven questions that consistently produce useful conversations. Each is built around process, effort, learning or enjoyment.

?What was the best bit of today?
?What did you find tricky?
?What did you learn today?
?What was the coach trying to teach you?
?Did anything click that hadn't before?
?What would you try differently next session?
?Did you have fun?
?Who did you train with?
?What did your body feel like at the end?
?What's one thing you'd like to get better at this week?
?Anything you want to ask your coach about?

Notice what these are not about. Not whether your child was fastest, not how they compared to a teammate, not what they did wrong. The questions invite reflection, not evaluation. Motivation lasts when a young athlete feels they own their progress — the questions hand the ownership back to them. They shape their own answer.

How to use this list

Don't run through all eleven at once — that turns a chat into an interview. Pick three that suit your child and your sport, and use the same three for a whole season. Swap in a new one only when an old one stops landing.

  • •Ask once, then go quiet. Let your child fill the silence.
  • •If the answer is one word, that's fine. Don't chase a longer one.
  • •Timing matters more than wording. After they've had a drink and a breather, not the instant they get in the car.
Worth avoiding

Five dead end questions

Just as useful as knowing what to ask is knowing what not to.

✕"How did you compare?"

Comparison with peers, especially friends, is a fast track to either complacency or anxiety. Neither helps.

✕"Why didn't you do X?"

This is coaching by question. If your child wanted technical correction, they'd have asked. The session is the place for technique, the car is not.

✕"Was [name] better than you?"

Specific peer comparison hurts more than general comparison. It puts the friendship in the same frame as the performance.

✕"How did that compare to last week?"

Good coaching focuses on progress over a season, not a single rep-to-rep comparison. Let the coach be the one tracking the numbers so you don't have to.

✕"Are you going to get picked / make the team?"

Selection anxiety is one of the biggest sources of dropout in youth sport. Let the result emerge from the training rather than making every conversation about the outcome.

If you find yourself wanting to ask any of these, that's fine and normal. The skill is noticing the urge — then choosing one of the questions above instead.

The pattern behind all five

John O'Sullivan, who has spent years studying why young athletes quit, found the same root cause behind almost every burnt-out or disengaged child. Sport had stopped being theirs, and started being something they performed for someone else's approval.

Every question on this page does that, in a small way — it hands the evaluation back to a parent. The five above are the most common offenders, not the only ones. If a question makes your child justify themselves, it probably belongs here too.

Progress you can see at home

What to look for over a training block

Test results and scoreboards matter — that's why coaches measure and re-test. But focused coaching produces changes you can see at home well before any formal report arrives.

1

Movement quality

An athlete who has worked on technique carries themselves differently. They stand taller, walk with a touch more spring. Watch their warm-up before any sport — it gets more deliberate and conscious.

2

Body confidence

Athletes who feel their body responding well take small physical risks they didn't take before — volunteering for the relay, getting into a tackle they'd have shied away from, trying the harder version of a drill.

3

Curiosity about the sessions

A few weeks in, an engaged child will be talking about a specific drill or technique, wanting to show you something they learned. Make a fuss of being shown — teaching it back to you is the deepest form of learning there is.

4

Independence

Packing their own kit, warming up without being told, or asking their coach a question themselves instead of routing it through you. Small as it sounds, that habit is worth more than any single skill they pick up.

5

Resilience after a hard session

Coming home from a tough session, eating dinner, and being in a good mood by bedtime is a signature of healthy sport-psychological development. If a session knocks your child sideways for more than an evening, that's worth a quiet word with their coach.

6

Enjoyment, plain and simple

The clearest marker of all: do they still want to go? A child developing well looks forward to sessions, even on a tired day. A dip in enthusiasm that lasts more than a week or two deserves a conversation with the coach, not just a pep talk at home.

You won't see all six every week, and that's normal. Development in young athletes comes in bursts, not a straight line. Looking for the pattern over a season tells you far more than any single week does.

If a test day or report comes back flat

When the numbers don't change the way you/they hoped

Some reports will sing — times will drop, jumps will climb, strength will be up across the board. Some won't. Some athletes plateau, or have a genuinely tough block of training because of a growth spurt, illness, or a hundred other reasons. If this is your child, here is what's worth remembering.

1

Testing measures one moment

Any single test day is a snapshot, not the full picture. A block of training doesn't always show its full effect by the time it ends. Many changes, particularly mechanics and movement quality, show two to three months later once the body has consolidated the training.

2

The knowledge doesn't vanish

A block of training, undertaken seriously by a young athlete, changes their understanding of how to train forever. They now know what a good warm-up feels like and what proper recovery is. That doesn't disappear because one number didn't move.

3

Your reaction is the lesson

Children take their cue from how adults respond to data they can't fully control. Receive a flat report calmly — "well, that's interesting, what did you take from it?" — and you've taught your child something durable about handling measured performance for life.

4

It's not a verdict on potential

Children mature physically at very different rates. A late developer can be a step behind at 13 and ahead at 16. Sport science has well-documented late bloomers in almost every discipline. One flat block of testing tells you nothing reliable about where a child ends up.

"The single best response to a flat report is the same response you'd give to a great one. Stay curious and warm, and let your child know the relationship is not built on results."
If you're stuck for words "That's interesting — what do you think made the difference this time?"
The bigger picture

Why this guide matters

The drop-out rate from organised youth sport in the UK rises sharply between ages 12 and 16. The single most consistent predictor of whether a child stays in sport is whether they enjoy it. Talent, time invested and geographical access all matter less than this one variable.

Youth athlete enjoyment is shaped by parents more than any other factor

Parents who are warm, curious, process-focused and present (without trying to coach) produce children who stay in sport longer, get more from it, and carry the habit of movement into adulthood.

This is not about being perfect. Every parent of a young athlete has had the journey home where they said the wrong thing — we have, countless times, with our own children and the athletes we coach. The work is noticing it, and adjusting the conversation next time. Whatever programme, club or team your child is part of, the way the two of you talk about it together is the rest of their relationship with sport.
